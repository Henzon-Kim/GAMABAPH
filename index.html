Gawad sa Manlilikha ng Bayan

The Gawad sa Manlilikha ng Bayan, also referred to as GAMABA, originates from the 1988 National Folk Artists Award organized by the Rotary Club of Makati-Ayala. These folk and traditional artists, collectively, represent the rich tapestry of our cultural heritage, transcending their origins to become integral to our national identity. As Filipinos, they showcase ancient customs, crafts, and ways of life, fostering an appreciation for our cultural essence. Through their work, they offer us a reflection of ourselves and our country, a vision we aspire to embody fully. Envisioned under R.A. 7355, a "Manlilikha ng Bayan" is defined as a citizen who practices a uniquely Filipino traditional art, possessing exceptional technical and artistic mastery that has been passed down and widely embraced by their community with the same level of proficiency.

Alonzo Saclag, a native of Lubuagan, Kalinga, is celebrated as a master of Kalingan performing arts, having learned solely out of his deep fascination for his culture. His mission revolves around preserving and promoting Kalingan traditions, which he achieves through community work and creative expressions like music, dance, ritualistic performance, and architecture. At a young age, he mastered instruments such as tongali (flute) and gangsa (gong). Recognized as a GAMABA awardee in 2000, Saclag's influence extends beyond his performances. He champions education, advocating for Kalinga culture in local schools and establishing the Kalinga Budong Dance Troupe. Notably, he leads efforts to preserve Kalinga heritage, converting an old capitol into a museum and establishing the Awichon Cultural Village, recognized by the NCCA as a focal point of Kalinga culture. (Dela Paz, 2019.; Saclag, 2017).

Tongali is a bamboo nose flute common among the northern indigenous people of Luzon. It is characterized by its four holes where it is played by using the nostrils and fingers, covering the other holes.

In the Cordillera region, the gangsa is a percussion instrument that is utilized for rites, festivities, and gatherings. It serves, in a way, to draw attention to everyone's presence as well as to honor the unity and companionship of those gathered in one location.

In Barangay Parang, Jolo, Sulu province, women weavers diligently create the pis syabit, a traditional cloth tapestry worn by the Tausug people. Among them is Darhata Sawabi, whose mastery of this art sustains her livelihood and independence. Despite facing challenges like the intricate weaving process and the disruption caused by conflict, Sawabi perseveres, preserving Tausug designs and passing on her knowledge to future generations. Her dedication ensures the continuity of this cultural tradition, while also empowering women in her community to earn a living through weaving. Because of her contributions in championing the Tausug artistry, she was given the Gamaba award in the year 2004 (Tobias, 2019).

The Mangyan literary tradition stands out for its use of the ambahan, a poetic form with seven-syllable lines conveying messages through metaphors. Ginaw Bilog, a Hanunoo Mangyan from Mindoro, is deeply immersed in this cultural heritage. He preserves ambahan poetry recorded on bamboo tubes and notebooks, passed down through generations using the pre-colonial script, Surat Mangyan. Ginaw shares these treasures with his community, promoting the roots of his culture at every opportunity. His dedication ensures that the ambahan poetry and other traditional art forms of the Mangyan people thrive, preserving a heritage nearly lost to colonial rule. Ginaw Bilog is celebrated for his efforts in preserving and promoting the elegant art of surat Mangyan and the ambahan. He was awarded the GAMABA title in the year 2003 (De Leon, 2019).

For Filipinos and his community, Ginaw Bilog's dedication to conserving Mangyan poetry—particularly the Surat Mangyan—will go on forever. His papers and bamboo tubes, which describe his efforts to preserve Ambahan and Surat Mangyan, significantly advance Philippine cultural heritage.

Masino Intaray, The Palawan people are the main indigenous groups in southern Palawan, along with the Batak and Tagbanwa. They have a sophisticated and diverse culture that is firmly based on spirituality and peace with the natural world. Their complex ceremonies, such as tambilaw and tinapay, place more emphasis on group sharing than on private property. In community life, music—especially that of the basic ensemble—is essential because it uses interlocking rhythms and harmonies to symbolize harmony and cooperation. Their close relationship with nature and poetic expression is further demonstrated by traditional arts like bagit and kulilal. Masino, a revered character from the Makagwa valley, is a prime example of proficiency in these customs; by his skill as a musician and storyteller, he acts as a protector of Palawan cultural legacy (De Leon, 2019).

Basal (Gong) instruments of Palawan are characterized into two types: the big gongs known as Agung, and the two smaller ringed gongs known as sanang. Consequently, the gimbal is the one that rests on a lateral platform. Overall, these types of gongs make up the basal instruments of Palawan.

Babarak or ring flute is a type of flute common in the indigenous tribes of Palawan. This group of end-blown flutes is characterized by a ring that surrounds the hole at the blowing end of the flute. This ring is usually made from a rattan strip.

Aruding is an instrument called "jaw's harp" in English, and it is made from a short segment of bamboo. It is placed close to the lips and blown to produce sound.

One of the largest Islamic Filipinos, the Magindanaons are talented in many artistic and musical genres. Situated primarily in particular communities within the province of Magindanao, they exhibit intricate skills in weaving, okir designs, and musical instruments such as the kulintang and kutyapi. However, proficiency in these skills is dwindling among newer generations of the tribe. This is evident, particularly with the two-stringed plucked lute known as the kutyapi. Conversely, the honorable Samaon Sulaiman serves as a beacon of indigenous culture as he is a master of kutyapi who has influenced people in his community with his exceptional playing and mentoring of this instrument. Samaon receives respect and appreciation for his commitment to conserving the kutyapi's melody despite his dual roles as an Imam and barber. Because of his contributions to the renewal of this dying culture, he was awarded the GAMABA recognition in the year 2011.

Kutyapi, a two-stringed instrument made from wood where one string denotes melody, while the other is for the drone.

Uwang Ahadas, born in Lamitan, Basilan in 1945, was immersed in his people's music from a young age, learning alongside his siblings. His talent blossomed as he mastered various instruments like the gabbang, agung, kwintangan kayu, and tuntungan before turning 20. In recognition of his contributions to enriching Yakan culture, he was awarded the GAMABA medal in 2000. Despite his failing eyesight, Ahadas remained focused on passing his knowledge to the next generation, teaching his children Yakan music from a young age. Darna, his daughter, is currently carrying on his heritage by instructing others in Yakan customs. Because he often introduced other Basilan communities to Yakan culture, Ahadas's influence went beyond Lamitan. Ahadas's lectures and annotations were recorded by his younger brother Rohas to guarantee the passing on of his knowledge (Bangsamoro Commission for the Preservation of Cultural Heritage - BARMM, 2022; Maranan, 2011).

Gabbang is an instrument also known as bamboo xylophone is often used as a musical instrument in the southern Philippines, particularly the Tausugs and Samas. It is an instrument typically used in dances that are played solo or accompanied by a biola.

The Kwintangan Kayu is a musical instrument composed of five wooden logs suspended horizontally, arranged from shortest to longest, with the shortest log closest to the ground. Following the rice planting, a platform without a roof is constructed high up in the tree branches

During the harvest season, the "Tuntungan," a wooden log drum with jar resonators, is played. This percussion instrument is renowned among the Yakans of Basilan.

Labnai Tumndan, known as Estelita later in life, was born in the montane hamlet of Mlasang, Sarangani, where Blaan culture thrived. Despite societal changes brought by Protestantism and political restructuring, Estelita remained dedicated to mat weaving, a tradition she learned from her childhood. Married to a supportive husband, she continued weaving mats, demonstrating exceptional skill and artistic vision. Recognized as a master weaver, she earned the endearing nickname "Princess" from her community, reflecting her esteemed position. Her serene demeanor and precise craftsmanship embody the essence of mat weaving, deeply rooted in Blaan heritage. Upper Lasang, her village, provides an ideal environment for the continuation of this tradition, supported by abundant plant materials and a strong cultural identity. Estelita's artistic legacy and personal humility highlight the enduring significance of mat weaving in Sarangani's coastal Blaan community (National Commission for Culture and the Arts, 2019).

Haja Amina Appi of Ungos Matata, Tandubas is a highly respected master mat weaver among the Sama indigenous community in Tawi-Tawi. Her mats are renowned for their intricate geometric patterns and vibrant colors, taking up to three months to complete. For context, Mat weaving is a skill passed down through the matrilateral line, exclusive to women in the Sama culture. The process involves harvesting, stripping, dyeing, and weaving pandan leaves into intricate designs. Aside from her mastery of their cultural craft, Haja Amina is also known for his more innovative and modern approach as she is open into using commercial dyes, creating her own color tints. While firmly rooted in the traditional customs of her tribe, she adeptly integrates contemporary designs, maintaining a focus on precision and instinct in crafting symmetrical patterns reminiscent of her cultural techniques. Her dedication to the craft has inspired her female descendants to continue the tradition, ensuring that mat weaving remains a thriving art form in her community (Bangsamoro Commission for the Preservation of Cultural Heritage - BARMM, 2022).

Lang Dulay, a master weaver of the Tbolis in Lake Sebu, South Cotabato, skillfully preserves her people's traditions through the intricate art of tnalak weaving. Despite the laborious process involving abaca fibers, Lang Dulay diligently creates intricate designs reflecting the stories and visions of her community. While modern designs may fetch higher prices, she remains dedicated to traditional techniques, aiming to honor her elders' songs through her textiles. Despite challenges, including commercialization and changing demands, Lang Dulay dreams of passing on her skills to future generations and establishing a school for women in her community. Her dedication to preserving her culture's artistry earned her recognition as a Gawad sa Manlilikha ng Bayan awardee, evoking tears of joy and a hopeful vision for the future of T'boli weaving (Tobias, 2019).

Born in Bansalan, Davao del Sur, Salinta Monon's life has been intertwined with the art of weaving since childhood, learning the traditional Tagabawa Bagobo techniques from her mother at a young age. Despite facing challenges such as marriage, motherhood, and the loss of her husband, she has continued to weave, becoming one of the last remaining Bagobo weavers in her community. Her dedication and skill have earned her a reputation for quality work and intricate designs. While facing modern challenges such as competition from machine-made fabrics, Salinta persists in preserving her cultural heritage through weaving. With a desire to pass on her knowledge to future generations, she dreams of establishing a dedicated weaving space and teaching younger women the art of weaving. Despite the dwindling numbers of weavers in the community, Salinta remains committed to upholding the rich tradition of Bagobo textiles, recognizing her role as a link to the past in a rapidly changing world.
